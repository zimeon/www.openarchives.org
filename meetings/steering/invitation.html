Open Archives. Background, mission, proposed governance, and next steps.

D Greenstein and C Lynch, 21 July 2000

The Open Archives Initiative has its genesis in an October 1999 meeting held in Santa Fe, New Mexico, under the sponsorship of CLIR, the Digital Library Federation (DLF), the Scholarly Publishing and Academic Resources Coalition, the Association of Research Libraries, and the Research Library of the Los Alamos National Laboratory. The meeting focused on the interoperation of "e-print archives" (collections of electronic journal articles and preprints), in particular on how e-print repositories could most easily share metadata about their holdings. It decided to pursue an approach that it adapted from the harvesting technique. In this approach, there are data providers and service providers. A data provider agrees to support a simple harvesting protocol and to provide extracts of its metadata in a common, minimal-level format in response to harvest requests. A service provider uses the harvest protocol to collect metadata from them. The service provider is then able to build user-oriented services, such as catalogs and portals to materials distributed across multiple e-print sites. The approach and its protocols were documented in the so-called Santa Fe Convention, along with preliminary ideas about acceptable use policies, registries and other issues.

 

Under the aegis of the DLF and with support from the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, the concepts developed in Santa Fe are being generalized into a model for research metadata harvesting that would apply to a wider range of digital resources of academic and scholarly interest. In addition to e-prints and electronic texts, such resources include science and social science data sets, visual materials, archival collections, geographic information system (GIS) data, sound and music, video, and any other type of resource for which metadata is typically created.

 

This work is still frankly experimental; one of the key issues facing us is to ensure that the current Santa Fe agreements receive technical validation and where necessary revision in the light of implementation experience specifically focused on the interoperation of e-print archives, and that while these evolving agreements are hospitable to also supporting experimentation with broader applications responsiveness to the original problem is retained.

 

The need to develop some light-weight governing structure as a means of promoting both implementation and experimentation were agreed at a second meeting of the Open Archives Initiative held in San  Antonio, Texas, in June 2000. The CNI and the DLF have agreed to contribute funds and resources to establish that governing structure.

 

Briefly, the governing structure envisaged for Open Archives Initiatives comprises a Steering Committee with access to modest administrative and other support.

 

The Steering Committee would be responsible for developing, maintaining, promoting adoption of, and publicizing the Open Archives as a technical framework. In fulfilling its mission, the Committee would:

q       provide strategic oversight of the framework’s maintenance, development, and public accessibility;

q       convene and monitor the work of any ad hoc technical or other advisory groups as may be appropriate

 

Support would also be available in the form of:

q       a well-managed web site www.openarchives.org supplying detailed up-to-date information about the technical framework;

q       editorial assistance as may be required to maintain that framework;

q       a mechanism capable of managing change requests and other technical queries from those involved or interested in implementing the framework;

q       administrative assistance as may be required to convene and document the work of the Steering Committee and any ad hoc advisory groups that it may convene; and

q       funding sufficient to support modest promotional activities.

 

 

The Steering Committee’s first order of business, to be conducted by conference call, is to:

 

q       review, revise where appropriate, and agree the governance structure as outlined above and its own roles and responsibilities;

q       consider recommendations emerging out of the San Antonio workshop, the upcoming Lisbon workshop being held in conjunction with the ECDL2000 meeting,  and DLF meetings to revise the Santa Fe Convention and, if necessary, nominating and convening an ad hoc technical advisory body to conduct that review in detail;

q       consider other next steps as may be appropriate in supplying the Open Archives initiative with the stability and public presence necessary to encourage its implementation in a variety of testbed initiatives.
