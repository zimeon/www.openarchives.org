Call for participation aimed at the further promotion of the preprint concept

Paul Ginsparg, Rick Luce, Herbert Van de Sompel - Los Alamos National Laboratory - April 1999

__________________

The purpose of this call is the mobilization of a core group to work towards achieving a universal service for non peer-reviewed scholarly literature. We think that important steps in such a direction can be taken by identifying or creating technologies and frameworks that stimulate:

  • the adoption of the preprint concept in all areas of scholarly research;
  • the integration of preprint services in the scholarly document system of scholarly journals, A&I services and libraries;
  • the creation of search and retrieval functionality for preprint archives that:
    • can be simultaneously useful for discipline-specific and cross-disciplinary purposes;
    • can be simultaneously useful for inter-institutional and intra-institutional purposes;
    • is user-friendly i.e. along the lines of established S&R methods;
    • cover meta-data, full-text, citation data.
__________________

In certain areas of scholarly activity, electronic preprint archives have become an established medium to communicate non peer-reviewed results of ongoing research. The trend began in high energy phyics in 1991 and since then, the centralized xxx preprint archive has become a global repository for research in physics. xxx has also incoporated mathematics, non-linear sciences and computer science. NCSTRL is a similar initiative, providing a point of access for technical reports in computer sciences, that are either submitted to the CoRR part of xxx or to decentral departmental archives that cooperate in the initiative. Archives in the NCSTRL initiative share the Dienst protocol, which enables the creation of library-like services that support searching and browsing the archive. Along the same lines, the RePEc initiative in economics provides authors with the option to submit working papers to the central EconWPA archive at Washington University or to a departmental archive, which supports an agreed upon protocol. This so called Guildford protocol guarantees interoperability between the RePEc archives and has enabled the creation of a variety of end-user services. Recently, NIH has expressed a strong interest in the establishment of a preprint initiative for biology. Slowly, the information industry is beginning to understand the potential of the preprint concept, regarding it either as an opportunity for collaborations, as a challenge or as a threat.

All the preprint initiatives share the same goal, which is to optimize scholarly communication by overcoming the barriers - financial, legal, ... - which the traditional framework has established. Still, some important barriers remain in place and action is required to promote the preprint concept beyond its current scope:

  • The preprint concept has only been adopted in distinct segments of scholarly research, and the success of existing initiatives has not yet generated comparable initiatives in all other areas. Institutionalization of the preprint approach could be instrumental in broadening the applicable range, since it allows for a natural extension of the prepublishing habit from early adopter disciplines to other research areas in multi-disciplinary institutions. Institutionalization has other benefits:
    • the addition of a level of author-authentication to submissions;
    • sensitivity about the institutional brand which might encourage institutions to impose a certain level of quality control;
    • the attribution of the archiving function to the party that should be best motivated to guarantee the longevity of its output;
    • the presence of know-how in institutional libraries that could guarantee a constant formal quality of the output;
    • the fact that institution based prepublishing can be seen as a straightforward extension of the ongoing creation of the institutional bibliography.
  • The existing preprint initiatives have been created from within certain disciplines. Important issues regarding cross-discipline interoperability remain to be addressed, in order to come to the creation of global multi-disciplinary end-user services. Since no segment of scholarly research can stand on its own, such services are crucial when aiming at the extension of both the user and the author base of the preprint environment.
  • A preprint is the first true public account of a research activity, but it is only the initial step in a life-cycle. Several versions of the preprint might be distributed, varying in document format and content. Peer-commentary or public reviewing can become connected to the preprint. And in many cases, peer-review will be added as an essential certificate of scientific value, be it in the form of a seperate peer-review document or a paper published in an established journal. Later, the preprint and/or paper may become cited, when it starts influencing other scholars' research. One very important aspect of the preprint concept is that the prepublished document receives an identifying number at the start of its life-cycle. Potentially, this early identification makes possible a framework to track the complete life-cycle of a published research activity. This is in contrast with traditional publishing, where identifiers are accorded somewhere further down the line, out of the reach of the research community and can only be obtained by "buying" it back again. Since such identifiers are becoming crucial to interrelate the output of research activities, this is a serious obstacle, strengthening the existing dependency on commercial services. Unfortunately, the existing preprint architectures have little support for "life after the preprint" that could allow for a smooth integration of several manifestations of a research publication, both free and commercial. The latter is essential for both scholarly communication as such and in convincing the information industry about the potential benefits of the preprint concept to their business.
These considerations - to which you can easily add numerous others - have urged us to initiate this call. Although we understand the dimensions of the problem, we propose a pragmatic approach, considering that such a strategy can sometimes lead to practical and convincing results.

The following are the action lines we are currently considering, and which are obviously open to discussion:

  • Alignment of existing preprint initiatives along a framework that:
    • is open to the creation of cross-disciplinary end-user services;
    • is open to the concept of "life after the preprint";
    • initially only serves to create a prototype system.
  • Promotion of the framework via the creation of a prototype demonstrating:
    • the universal service notion for interoperable preprint initiatives;
    • the openness of the framework for the addition of "life after the preprint" services, both free and commercial;
    • global dissemination of a multi-authored paper on the initiative (cf the copyright paper) to academic institutions, library organizations, authors, readers;
  • Submission of a project proposal aimed at the development and the promotion of a solid framework for a Universal Preprint Service (UPS).
