Andrew Albanese Contributing Editor DIGITAL LIBRARIANS HOLD FIRST MEETING OF UPS INITIATIVE Digital librarians involved with the Universal Preprint Service Initiative (UPS), a project with far-reaching implications for the future of scholarly communications, came away from their first meeting in Santa Fe, NM, having made significant strides toward creating "interoperability" among digital archives throughout the world. The ambitious project, which brought together the world's leading digital information specialists, seeks to bring "some level of compatibility between [digital archives]," says Herbert Van de Sompel, one of the initiative's organizers. This is the key to making electronic archives more central to scholarly research. "Scholarly communication via digital archives," says Van de Sompel (of the University of Ghent, Belgium), "can only become a true part of scholarly communication if there is some kind of interoperability between them." According to Van de Sompel, a set of "architectural, technical, and organizational recommendations," to be known as the Santa Fe Conventions, were set forth and adopted by the digital archives with representatives at the meeting. "As a result of that," says Van de Sompel, "we can expect the emergence of a multi-disciplinary search engine for eprint material originating from those existing archives. And as more existing archives implement the recommendations, the reach of such a search engine will grow." But just how big of an impact will the UPS Inititaive have on the changing landscape of scholarly communications? "That is a very difficult question to answer, since none of us have the ability to predict the future," says Van de Sompel. "But I somehow have the feeling that our Initiative has set some things on fire.... [it is] is one of an ever increasing number of initiatives that strive for a change in scholarly communication. At some point a convergence of several of these initiatives will get things right.
